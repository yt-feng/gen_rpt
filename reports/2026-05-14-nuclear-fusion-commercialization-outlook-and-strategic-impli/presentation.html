Key highlights

The research narrows the agenda to a few high-conviction issues

1

Nuclear fusion, long considered a distant energy source, is now attracting unprecedented private and public investment, with over $6 billion in private funding and major projects like ITER, SPARC, and DEMO advancing. However, commercialization remains a decade or more away due to unresolved engineering challenges, particularly in plasma confinement, materials science, and tritium breeding. This report assesses the technology readiness, economic viability, regulatory landscape, and strategic implications of fusion. Key findings: fusion could achieve cost parity with renewables by 2050 under optimistic scenarios, but faces risks from regulatory gaps and tritium scarcity. Strategic implications include potential disruption of fossil fuel markets, new geopolitical dependencies, and a need for early investment in supply chains and regulatory frameworks. Decision-makers should monitor milestones, engage in pilot projects, and prepare for a phased integration of fusion into the energy mix.
